The ICP — Defined & Sourced

Where this ICP came from

This ICP was built across three sequential research documents. Each attribute below traces to a specific finding. This section exists so any reader of this document understands the full reasoning — not just the conclusion.

The SSD ICP — DTC Digital Health
A seed–Series A DTC digital health startup building a consumer-facing app or platform with an AI or data layer, founded by a technical or clinical founder who lacks in-house design capacity, having raised $1–20M, needing to close the gap between their investor pitch and their product experience before their next round.
StageSeed – Series A (occasionally Series B if design gap is acute)
Founder TypeTechnical (ML/AI/eng), clinical (MD/PhD), or domain-expert operator — not design-first
ProductConsumer-facing app or platform (not purely B2B clinical software or biotech)
AI/Data LayerHas an AI story in their pitch or product — personalization, diagnostics, coaching, data insights
Design GapNo in-house designer or design lead; design is outsourced, absent, or under-resourced
Timing Window0–18 months post-funding close — the active vendor evaluation window before teams scale internally
GeographyPrimarily US (SF, NYC, Boston) but distributed — DTC digital health is remote-first by nature
Core Problem to Solve"Our AI story is in the pitch deck. The product doesn't feel as intelligent or trustworthy as what we promised investors."
How each ICP attribute was determined
Seed–Series A stage
Rock Health Q1 2026 data showed average deal size hit $36.7M — but deal count dropped, meaning fewer companies are getting larger checks. Series A/B is where design investment first becomes available. Pre-seed companies lack budget; Series C+ companies have in-house design teams. The $1–20M raise window is when founders are actively vendor-evaluating. Confirmed by the funding dates on this list — 12 of 13 high-fit companies raised in 2024–2025.
SourceHealth & Wellness Industry Brief → Market Context section. Rock Health Q1 2026 funding data.
Technical or clinical founder
Rock Health reported that 9 of 11 megadeals in H1 2025 went to AI-enabled health startups — meaning the funded founder archetype is ML engineers, biotech researchers, and clinicians building AI products. The brand landscape analysis confirmed this: K Health (clinical AI), Function Health (MD co-founder), Superpower (technical ops founders), Mito Health (YC engineers) all show the same pattern. Non-design founding teams produce design gaps by default — they optimize for the problem they know.
SourceIndustry Brief → AI as table stakes. Brand Landscape → Brand Gaps section (AI Promise Gap). Confirmed per-company in founder background column above.
Consumer-facing product required
SSD's service model (Launch/Grow/Scale tiers) is optimized for consumer-facing digital products — brand systems, onboarding flows, dashboards, app UI. Purely B2B clinical software (EHR integrations, hospital ops tools) requires different design competencies (enterprise UX, complex data workflows) that SSD doesn't currently position around. The DTC consumer health segment also aligns with SSD's existing case studies (Kenchan Ramen = DTC ecomm; Funding Solutions = consumer-facing fintech dashboard). The ICP narrows to consumer-facing to ensure SSD is selling into a problem it demonstrably solves.
SourceSSD Business Overview (service tiers). Existing case studies (Kenchan, Funding Solutions). Industry Brief → ICP Direction section.
AI or data layer in product
The Brand Landscape Analysis found that 18 of 25 companies in this validation list claim AI personalization in their positioning. Fewer than 5 have design that reflects it — this gap was named the "AI Promise Gap" and identified as the single most common failure across the landscape. An AI/data product creates a specific design challenge: how do you make machine intelligence feel legible, trustworthy, and personalized to a consumer? This is SSD's most defensible pitch because it requires both domain fluency (understanding what the AI actually does) and consumer UX sophistication (making it feel human). Generalist agencies rarely have both.
SourceDigital Health Brand Landscape → Brand Gaps section (AI Promise Gap, Data Without Legibility). Stats strip above: 18/25 companies on this list have AI framing.
No in-house design capacity
Assessed per company via LinkedIn job posting signals, website design quality relative to funding stage, and press coverage patterns (journalists describing what the product does but not how it looks = signal of design underinvestment). The pattern is structural, not incidental: technical and clinical founders optimize for what they know. Design is the last hire, not the first. This was confirmed by the brand landscape — K Health, LifeMD, and Teladoc all show the same under-design pattern despite having capital to invest. The gap persists because no one on the founding team knows what good looks like.
SourceBrand Landscape → Visual Convergence gap. Per-company design signal column in table above. LinkedIn job posting analysis (methodology described in "Where companies come from" section of ICP validation response).
SSD fit: existing strengths
Three SSD-specific reasons this ICP is the best fit — not just a good market. (1) Fintech/SaaS pattern transfer: designing trust into sensitive data interactions, making complex information legible, building systems for regulated industries — all directly transferable from SSD's Funding Solutions work. (2) Silk + Sonder background: consumer subscription retention, habit formation UX, physical × digital integration — all live design experience in adjacent consumer health territory. (3) SSD's brand voice pillars (Strategic, Human, Bold, Insightful) are a natural match for a segment where the core product tension is "clinical rigor vs. consumer warmth" — exactly the register SSD already operates in.
SourceSSD Business Overview + Brand Identity docs. Patti's Silk + Sonder case study (patriciacapiral.com). Industry Brief → Strategic Recommendation section. Brand Landscape → Part 4 Strategic Rec.

Strategic Notes

What this list tells you

ICP Hypothesis: Confirmed
The pattern holds across all 13 high-fit companies: clinical or technical founder, AI pitch, fresh seed–Series A capital, no design-first team member. Fay, Mito Health, Lōvu, Clara Health, Daymark — all fit this profile precisely. The hypothesis is validated.
The Timing Signal
Post-funding, pre-scale is the SSD window. Fay ($50M Series A), Berry Street ($50M Series A), Nourish ($70M Series B), Culina ($7.9M Series A) all raised in 2024–2025. Founders are now allocating that capital — design agencies are evaluated in the first 3–6 months post-close. Timing outreach to funding announcements is the highest-ROI tactic.
Shared Design Problem
The single most common gap across score-4 and score-5 companies: "AI is in the pitch but not in the product experience." This is SSD's most credible entry line. 18 of 25 companies have some AI framing in their positioning. Fewer than 5 have design that reflects it.
Two-Sided Marketplace Pattern
Nourish, Fay, Berry Street all run two-sided care marketplaces (patient + provider/RD). This is a distinct design problem: two separate user types, two interface contexts, one coherent brand. This pattern will repeat across the category. SSD should have a point of view on it.
Exclusions Are Instructive
Flo (1B+ valuation), Omada (IPO), Talkspace (public) are the right exclusions. They validate the market — they don't indicate SSD should pursue them. The lesson: this segment produces real exits. SSD's clients at seed–Series A today are 3–5 years from those outcomes.
Recommended First Contacts
Based on fit score + timing + design gap acuity: Mito Health, Clara Health, Lōvu Health, Daymark Health, Teal Health. These five are early enough that SSD can genuinely shape the product design direction — not just polish what already exists. They're also all post-funding with budget.
