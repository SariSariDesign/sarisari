## Post B — Design Principles Are Transferable (Post First)

**Audience:** Peers, potential clients, founders
**Goal:** Establish design expertise, show fintech depth translates broadly, soft CTA for UX review

We spent Q1 deep in fintech. Audited landing pages, dashboards, onboarding flows, KYC screens, compliance pages. Every company had the same problems:

• The trust signals looked like an afterthought.
• The dashboards all felt like the same template.
• The onboarding flows were leaking users at the exact same steps.

Here's what surprised me: none of these are fintech problems. They're growth-stage problems.

Building trust with first-time users? Every B2B product is asking someone to hand over sensitive data or make a buying decision. Differentiating when your product looks like everyone else's? That's every startup in a crowded category. Turning compliance from a friction point into a competitive advantage? That applies anywhere that regulation affects the user experience.

We niched down into fintech because we kept seeing the same gaps. Going deep in one vertical forced us to build a sharper lens for the patterns that actually matter: where trust breaks down, where users drop off, and where design can close the gap.

We set out to learn one industry and came out with a playbook that works across all of them.

What's a lesson from your niche that turned out to be way more universal than you expected?

## Post C — 120 Companies, 0 New Clients (Post Second)

**Audience:** Peers, founders, agency owners
**Goal:** Community building, invite advice from agency owners, build in public transparency

We researched 120 companies this quarter. Reached out to about 55. Closed 0 new clients.

Is there something we're missing?

All of our Q1 revenue came from one existing client. Here's how the quarter went.

1. We ran an experiment.

We niched into fintech after looking at our current clients and seeing the same trust and polish problems repeat across companies. Spent the quarter testing whether LinkedIn cold outreach to Series B startups could generate new business for a productized design studio.

We've got some signal from ~120 companies. Not enough to conclude anything yet — so we're keeping at it.

2. The top of the funnel held. Something broke further down.

Of those 120, about a quarter didn't pass our qualification research. For the ones that did, we built a structured outreach process: research the company, send a connection request, and once connected, nurture before ever mentioning what we do. In practice that meant sharing a UX observation about their product, starting a conversation, and only moving toward a call once there was real back and forth. Zero hard pitches. The question we're sitting with: is the nurture step the right instinct, or is it slowing us down?

3. Our warmest leads came from people who already knew us.

All of our revenue this quarter came from an existing client. The closest I got to a new conversation was with people from past work.

My hypothesis: at this stage, existing relationships outperform cold outreach. But I'm not sure if that's true for everyone or just where we are right now.

Agency founders — what worked for you in the early days: going wide with outreach, or going deep with the people who already know you?
