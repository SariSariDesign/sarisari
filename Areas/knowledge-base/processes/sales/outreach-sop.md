# Outreach SOP — LinkedIn Cold Outreach

## Overview

LinkedIn is our primary outreach channel for prospecting Series A/B fintech decision-makers. This SOP covers the process from targeting through to booking a discovery call.

Our approach is grounded in "Give First" and "Observed Not Assumed" frameworks (Josh Braun's outbound methodology): lead with genuine value, base observations on actual research rather than assumptions, and earn the right to pitch through a multi-touch warm sequence.

## Target Profile

- **Title:** Head of Product, VP Product, CPO, VP of Ops, or Founder/CEO at smaller companies
- **Company stage:** Series A or Series B (recently raised capital)
- **Industry:** Fintech primarily; B2B SaaS with compliance needs as secondary
- **Company size:** 5–100 employees
- **Signal:** Recently raised capital, hiring for product/design roles, launching new product features, or showing rapid growth (high YoY metrics)

## Outreach Priority Order

Not all outreach is equal. Prioritize in this order:

1. **Warm referrals from existing clients.** These convert 3–5x vs cold outreach. Always ask satisfied clients if they know others who could benefit. This is the highest-leverage activity.
2. **Warm leads with existing context.** Partners, past conversations, people who've engaged with your content. Re-engage with a specific reason tied to their current situation.
3. **Cold LinkedIn outreach at volume.** Target: 10–15 new connection requests per week, plus 3–5 value-first DMs to existing connections who haven't been engaged yet.
4. **Inbound from content/website.** Important but deprioritized until outbound volume is consistent. Don't use content creation as a reason to avoid outreach reps.

## Connection Request Strategy

**Critical rule: The connection note is NOT a pitch.** Its only job is to get the accept. The pitch lives in Step 3 or later of the follow-up sequence. Connection notes that read as soft pitches (even conditional ones like "if design becomes a bottleneck") signal sales intent and reduce acceptance rates.

**Good connection note structure:**

1. Reference something specific about them or their company (a post they wrote, a product launch, a funding round)
2. State a genuine reason for connecting (shared interest, mutual connection, relevant experience)
3. No ask, no pitch, no mention of services

**Bad signals to avoid:**

- "If you ever need..." (conditional pitch)
- "We help companies like yours..." (vendor language)
- "I'd love to share how we..." (pitch framing)
- Mentioning your agency, services, or rates anywhere in the note

## Follow-Up Sequence (6-Step)

This is the full sequence from connection to pitch. The key principle: the pitch is earned, not forced. If it doesn't happen naturally, move on.

1. **Step 1 — Connect (Day 0):** Send connection request with a zero-pitch note. Wait for accept. If no accept after 2 weeks, move on.

2. **Step 2 — Content Engagement (Days 3–10 after accept):** Engage with their content on LinkedIn. Leave substantive comments that add to the conversation — not likes, not "Great post!" Show you're paying attention to their domain. This builds familiarity in their feed before you ever appear in their inbox. Do NOT send a DM at this stage. Do NOT introduce Sari Sari. Your LinkedIn profile does the passive identity work — if they click through, they'll find you.

3. **Step 3 — Observation DM (Day ~14):** Send a targeted DM built around a specific, researched observation about their product, company, or market situation. This is the first and highest-leverage message in the sequence. Under 50 words. No mention of Sari Sari, no services pitch, no ask for a call. Close with a soft, low-friction CTA like "Relevant to you?" — not "Are you free for a call?" See "Pre-DM Research: What to Look For" and "Crafting the Observation DM" below for how to find and frame the observation.

4. **Step 4 — Build Rapport (if they respond):** If they engage with the DM, continue the conversation in their domain. Ask questions, share relevant thinking, be a peer. Do NOT introduce Sari Sari until the conversation earns it naturally. This may take multiple exchanges.

5. **Step 5 — Second Value Drop (Day ~21, if no response to Step 3):** Send one more message with a different observation or resource. This should NOT reference the first DM or ask "did you see my message?" Just offer value again from a different angle. If no response after this, stop messaging. You can continue engaging with their content passively.

6. **Step 6 — Pitch (only if earned):** If the conversation develops genuine rapport and an opening appears, introduce what Sari Sari does in context. Frame it as relevant to something they've already told you about, not as a generic pitch. The goal is a discovery call, not a close.

**Hard rules:**

- Do not send more than 2 unreturned DMs. Ever.
- Do not send a pitch before Step 6.
- Do not spend more than 2 weeks refining a single message. Send it and move on to the next prospect. Volume and consistency beat perfection.

## Framing for the Contact's Role

The value-first DM (Step 3) must speak to what the contact actually owns — not just what you noticed.

**If the contact is in Product/Design:** Frame observations around user experience, conversion, onboarding friction, or product clarity. They own these outcomes.

**If the contact is in Ops/Compliance:** Don't give marketing advice. Frame observations around how their product builds (or fails to build) trust with compliance buyers. Example: "When compliance teams evaluate a new vendor, there's usually an internal vetting process before anyone even books a demo" — this speaks to their world, even if the observation is about the website.

**If the contact is a Founder/CEO:** Frame around growth, market positioning, or competitive advantage. They care about all functions but respond to strategic framing.

**General rule:** If your observation is about something they don't control (e.g., the website, and they're in Ops), reframe it in terms of the outcome they do care about.

## Pre-DM Research: What to Look For

Before writing the Step 3 observation DM, do a 10–15 minute research pass on the contact and company. Work through these sources in order: LinkedIn team page → LinkedIn activity of the contact → open job postings → company homepage → recent news. Stop when you find one strong signal. Do not try to combine multiple signals into a single DM.

Each signal below maps to a specific type of observation. The DM fragments are starting points — always rewrite them to match the contact's role and the specific details you found.

**1. Open designer job req, 60+ days old**
Where to find it: LinkedIn Jobs, their careers page, job boards.
What it tells you: They know they need design capacity, haven't solved it, and the backlog is building.
DM fragment: *"[Company] has been hiring a product designer for [X] months. That gap usually means [specific flow — onboarding, compliance, checkout] is carrying debt that compounds the longer it sits. Relevant to you?"*

**2. Engineer-heavy team, no designer on the org chart**
Where to find it: LinkedIn company page → People tab, filter by title.
What it tells you: They're building without design input — usually visible in the product even from screenshots or the marketing site.
DM fragment: *"[Company] is [X] engineers deep with no dedicated designer. That ratio tends to show up first in the [onboarding / trust / conversion] layer — the part that matters most to buyers who don't know you yet. Relevant to you?"*

**3. Generic homepage headline — differentiator buried**
Where to find it: Their homepage, above the fold.
What it tells you: Their technical moat isn't translating into positioning. Common in infrastructure and B2B fintech where engineers wrote the copy.
DM fragment: *"[Company]'s actual differentiator — [X] — doesn't show up until [where it appears]. The headline reads like it could belong to any [payment / lending / banking] company. Relevant to you?"*

**4. Compliance or trust signals missing or thin**
Where to find it: Homepage footer, Trust/Security page, pricing page, onboarding screenshots.
What it tells you: For fintech, compliance credibility is a purchase decision factor — especially in B2B. If it's not surfaced, enterprise buyers are doing extra work to feel safe before they ever reach out.
DM fragment: *"[Company]'s compliance posture isn't visible until deep in the site. For enterprise buyers evaluating a new vendor, that vetting usually happens before they ever book a demo. Relevant to you?"*
Note: This signal plays directly to Sari Sari's positioning in fintech compliance UX — it's one most generalist agencies won't catch.

**5. Single conversion path ("Get in touch" only)**
Where to find it: Homepage CTA, nav, pricing page.
What it tells you: There's no mid-funnel path for buyers who are evaluating but not ready. Enterprise sales cycles are multi-touch — this is a structural gap in the conversion layer.
DM fragment: *"The only way to engage [Company] right now is a sales call. Most enterprise buyers visit 3–5 times before reaching out — there's nothing to bring them back between visits. Relevant to you?"*

**6. Contact posted publicly about a scaling challenge**
Where to find it: LinkedIn activity tab on the contact's profile.
What it tells you: They've named a pain themselves. This is the highest-quality signal on the list because you're responding to something they chose to say, not inferring from their site.
DM fragment: Reference what they said directly. *"Saw your post about [specific thing]. That tension between [shipping velocity / team size / compliance pressure] and [design quality / trust / conversion] usually shows up first in [specific place]. Relevant to you?"*

**7. Recent product launch with rough UX visible in screenshots**
Where to find it: Product Hunt, their blog, LinkedIn announcements, App Store screenshots.
What it tells you: They shipped something functional but visually rough — often because there was no designer in the loop.
DM fragment: *"[Feature] looks like it's doing real work. The [onboarding / flow / screen] in the launch screenshots is carrying a lot — that kind of thing starts to show in activation rates at scale. Relevant to you?"*

**8. Compliance regulation change in their vertical**
Where to find it: CFPB, OCC, or relevant regulatory body news; match to their product category (lending, payments, banking, insurance).
What it tells you: External pressure is creating urgency they didn't choose. Being the person who flagged it first positions you as informed, not opportunistic — if the framing is right.
DM fragment: *"With [regulation / rule change] coming into effect, the companies that get ahead of the UX on [KYC / disclosure / consent flows] tend to absorb the change with less friction. [Company] is in the window right now. Relevant to you?"*

## Crafting the Observation DM (Step 3)

**What makes a good observation:**

- Connects to revenue or growth (not cosmetic issues)
- Speaks to the contact's domain and role
- Is framed as rearranging existing assets, not creating something new (lower perceived effort = more likely to act)
- References something specific you found in your research — not a generic pain point that applies to any company
- Ends with a soft CTA: "Relevant to you?" or equivalent — not an ask for a call

**What to avoid:**

- Minor UI bugs or cosmetic issues (these don't warrant a message from a stranger)
- Observations that require heavy qualification or backstory to make sense
- Anything that sounds like you ran an automated audit and are spamming the results
- Questions asking the prospect to confirm your hypothesis ("Are you struggling with X?") — make a statement, not a screening question
- "We noticed X, we can help with Y" — this is a pitch disguised as an observation
- Mentioning Sari Sari, your services, or the audit tool in this message

**Format:**

- Under 50 words total
- One observation only — do not stack multiple findings
- Close with a soft, low-friction CTA ("Relevant to you?" or "Does this match what you're seeing?")
- No warm opener needed if the observation is strong enough — get to the point

## UX Audits as a Tool

We have the capability to run detailed UX audits on prospect websites. These are valuable but must be used at the right stage.

**Do NOT use the audit as an opening tool.** Leading with "I found problems on your website" in a cold DM feels adversarial and presumptuous, even if the findings are valid. Audit findings tend to over-weight cosmetic issues (triple-rendered buttons, staging UI leaks) using heuristic severity frameworks that don't reflect functional impact.

**DO use the audit as a closing tool.** Once a discovery call is booked or a conversation has developed genuine interest, the audit becomes a powerful asset. It demonstrates depth, thoroughness, and gives the prospect something tangible to evaluate before committing.

**Selective sharing:** If the audit surfaces a genuinely impactful finding (something connected to revenue, not cosmetics), that single insight can be the basis for the Step 3 DM — but reframed as a casual observation, not as "we audited your site."

## Booking Demos at Prospect Companies

**Do NOT book a fake demo** at a prospect's company to gather product intel for your outreach. At small companies (under 100 people), a founder or senior team member often runs demos. It burns trust, wastes their time, and word travels fast in fintech. If your outreach works, you'll gain product insight through the relationship naturally. If you want to see their product, be transparent about who you are.

## Weekly Cadence Targets

- **10–15** new connection requests sent
- **3–5** value-first DMs to existing connections
- **Content engagement** on 5+ prospects' posts (substantive comments)
- **Pipeline review** in Notion tracker: update every prospect's status weekly. You should be able to answer "how many prospects are in each stage?" at any time.

## Tracking

Outreach pipeline is tracked in Notion. Each prospect gets a record with: name, company, title, stage in sequence, last action date, next action, and notes. Update weekly at minimum.

## Key Lessons Learned

- Short, zero-pitch connection notes significantly outperform longer notes that hint at services. The note's only job is to get the accept.
- Volume and consistency beat message perfection. Don't spend weeks refining one DM. Send it, learn, iterate across many prospects.
- Warm outreach and referrals consistently outperform high-volume cold sequences. Always prioritize warm paths first.
- Frame observations for the contact's role, not just what you noticed. A VP of Compliance doesn't own the website — speak to compliance buyer psychology instead.
- UX audit findings are a closing tool, not an opening tool. Leading with "I found bugs" in a cold DM is adversarial.
- Cosmetic issues (UI rendering bugs, staging leaks) don't warrant outreach. Only lead with observations that connect to revenue or growth.
- Social proof gaps (e.g., "you don't have testimonials on your homepage") require too much qualification in a cold DM and risk sounding like you didn't do your homework. Check thoroughly before claiming something is missing.
- "Rearrange what you already have" framing works better than "create something new" — it lowers perceived effort and shows you understand their existing assets.
- Do not invest in LinkedIn automation tools that violate LinkedIn ToS. The risk of account restriction outweighs the efficiency gain.

## Deprioritized Activities

These are valid but should not take time away from outreach volume:

- AI education content series / ManyChat funnel (different audience, long-term play)
- Further website refinement (good enough — focus on driving traffic to it)
- Custom CRM infrastructure / automated lead scraping (premature at current stage)
