Methodology

This audit evaluates the Talos marketing site against 8 static UX dimensions (50+ criteria) from Sari Sari Design's UX Audit Reference Checklist, prioritized for enterprise SLG motion in institutional crypto infrastructure.

Evaluation Lens

Talos sells into institutional buyers who evaluate vendors through a rigorous procurement lens — credibility signaling, specificity, and ease-of-path-to-demo matter more than volume of features. This audit prioritizes how well the marketing site compresses complex institutional messaging into a clear path for a Head of Trading or CTO to self-qualify and request a demo.

Why This Matters

For a growth-stage infrastructure player competing with FalconX and B2C2, every friction point between landing and demo request is measurable lost pipeline — an institutional crypto deal is typically $250K–$2M+ ACV. Marketing site clarity disproportionately determines whether a vetting committee at a prospective bank or hedge fund moves Talos forward in the decision.

Top 5 Findings

Selected for business impact, evidence quality, and relevance to Talos's growth stage and enterprise GTM.

1
Content Hierarchy & CopywritingMajor

The strongest proof on the site — "$727B in volume" — is buried mid-page

The statistic "$727B in volume since inception" appears in the By the Numbers section well below the fold. It is the single most specific, verifiable, and credibility-building claim on the entire site, yet the hero uses a generic 5-word adverb stack ("at scale. anywhere. safely. securely. optimally.") instead.

Why this matters for Talos: Institutional buyers have ~5 seconds to decide whether a vendor is credible enough to explore further. A Head of Trading evaluating 3-4 crypto infrastructure vendors will remember a number like $727B — they will not remember adverbs. In a crowded category (FalconX, B2C2, Fireblocks), this stat is the shortest path to a defensible credibility claim.

COPY-08 Proof and Credibility in Copy

Recommendation

Elevate the stat to the hero headline or sub-headline. Example: "The institutional trading platform powering $727B in crypto volume." Let the specific, verifiable number do the work that the generic adverb stack is currently failing to do.

Effort: LowImpact: High
2
Conversion Rate OptimizationMajor

"Request a demo" is the only engagement path — mid-funnel visitors have nowhere to go

The CTA "Request a demo" is repeated 5+ times across the homepage with no variation. There is no mid-funnel option for a treasurer, COO, or analyst doing early research who isn't ready to commit to a sales call.

Why this matters for Talos: Enterprise buying committees include people at very different stages of intent. A CTO may be demo-ready; their head of compliance is not. Forcing every visitor into a single binary action suppresses pipeline capture from the 70%+ of visitors who are in exploration mode. Mid-funnel capture (gated playbook, product video, "talk to a product expert") brings these visitors into nurture instead of losing them.

CRO-04 Engagement Path Variety

Recommendation

Keep "Request a demo" as the hero and final-section CTA. Replace 2 of the mid-page CTAs with mid-funnel options: a gated institutional crypto playbook, a 90-second platform walkthrough video, or "Talk to a product expert" (signals exploration vs. purchase).

Effort: MediumImpact: High
3
Content Hierarchy & CopywritingMajor

Hero headline could describe any competitor — no differentiation

The hero reads "We empower institutions to trade and manage digital assets" with a 5-word adverb stack beneath. Every word of this could appear verbatim on a FalconX, Fireblocks, or B2C2 homepage. Nothing in the headline is uniquely true of Talos.

Why this matters for Talos: In a crowded institutional crypto infrastructure market, the hero's job is to answer "why this vendor, not the others?" in 5 seconds. Generic positioning invites the buyer to treat you as interchangeable with the others — which is a losing position against better-funded incumbents.

COPY-03 Headline Specificity / CRO-05 Value Proposition Clarity

Recommendation

Replace the adverb stack with a single specific, defensible claim grounded in what's actually true only for Talos — volume handled, number of venues connected, or a customer-outcome data point. Lead with what the competition can't legitimately copy.

Effort: LowImpact: High
4
Visual & Brand DesignMinor

Awards & credentials hidden in a collapsed accordion

Recognition from Risk.net, Hedgeweek, Digital Banker, and Forbes is collapsed by default. These are institutional-grade third-party validations — particularly Risk.net, which carries weight in trading ops procurement conversations.

Why this matters for Talos: Institutional buyers weigh third-party validation heavily during vetting. Hiding it behind a click reduces the probability it factors into the decision. A Risk.net mention alone can shorten a vetting cycle by meaningfully reducing perceived risk.

CRO-06 Trust Signal Placement / VD-08 Industry-Appropriate Trust Signaling

Recommendation

Expand the awards by default, or surface the top 3-5 institutionally-recognized awards inline near the testimonial section. Keep the accordion only for the long tail of recognition.

Effort: LowImpact: Medium
5
Information ArchitectureMinor

Nav splits "Solutions" and "Clients" — two models presented as peers

The top navigation offers parallel paths: "Solutions" (by product: Trading Platform, RFQ, etc.) and "Clients" (by persona: Asset Managers, Hedge Funds, etc.). Both routes lead to overlapping content, forcing users to pick between two mental models at the top level.

Why this matters for Talos: With 8+ personas and 7+ products, the matrix creates decision friction at the navigation layer. Enterprise buyers often wear multiple hats (a CTO evaluating for both a hedge fund and a market-making desk) and may bounce rather than pick. A single primary taxonomy is easier to navigate at the homepage level.

IA-01 Navigation Clarity / IA-04 Persona Routing

Recommendation

Consolidate into a single primary taxonomy (persona is typically best for enterprise SLG) and use Solutions as a sub-taxonomy within each persona landing page. Alternatively, add a 1-line hint label: "Solutions — by what it does" / "Clients — by who uses it."

Effort: HighImpact: Medium
