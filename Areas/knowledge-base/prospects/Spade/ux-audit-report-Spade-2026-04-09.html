Evaluation Lens

Spade competes in a crowded transaction enrichment category where Ntropy, Heron, and Atomic pitch similar stories to the same buyers. Spade's best wedge is its logo wall (Stripe, Cash App Pay, Mercury, Robinhood, PayPal, Coinbase) — which reads like a category winner. The audit prioritizes whether the site actually makes that wedge the hero story.

Why This Matters

Enterprise infrastructure buyers short-list based on 60-second homepage scans. Spade's logo wall is arguably the strongest trust signal in its category — if it isn't hero-level, the company is throwing away its biggest lead. Every moment spent explaining "what is transaction enrichment" to a buyer who already knows is a moment not spent closing.

Top 5 Findings

Ranked by estimated impact on pipeline conversion.

1
Content Hierarchy & CopywritingMajor

Your strongest claim — "1 in 3 U.S. startups" — isn't in the hero

Buried in a Mercury testimonial: "1 in 3 U.S. startups rely on transaction data enriched by Spade." That is a near-monopoly claim — the strongest possible category signal an infrastructure company can make. It appears mid-page, not above the fold. Paired with it: "<50ms P99 enrichment latency" — your technical trust anchor — also buried.

Why this matters for Spade: In infrastructure, market share IS the product. Buyers are choosing who will still be running their pipeline in 5 years. "1 in 3 startups" shuts down that concern in a sentence. Every visitor who leaves the homepage without seeing it is a preventable loss.

COPY-04 Information Hierarchy / CRO-05 Value Proposition Clarity

Recommendation

Promote both numbers into the hero. Direction: "Transaction enrichment for 1 in 3 U.S. fintech startups. <50ms P99 latency. Trusted by Stripe, Mercury, and Cash App." Lead with proof you already have instead of category-generic framing.

Effort: LowImpact: High
2
Trust SignalsMajor

Best-in-class logo wall, under-leveraged in the narrative

Spade's customer list — Stripe, Cash App Pay, Mercury, Robinhood, PayPal, Coinbase, Monzo, Afterpay, Airwallex, Current, Navan — is arguably the strongest in the fintech data category. These logos appear on the page but aren't woven into the hero narrative itself.

Why this matters for Spade: For enterprise infra, logo quality IS the product. A new buyer's first thought is "who else uses this?" If the answer only appears in a strip below the fold and never in the hero's words, the wedge is being under-monetized by the copy.

CRO-07 Social Proof Depth / CRO-06 Trust Signal Placement

Recommendation

Move the logo wall directly below the hero with a bold framing line: "The infrastructure behind the next generation of finance — used by Stripe, Cash App, Mercury, Robinhood, and 50+ others." Let the logos carry the trust work.

Effort: LowImpact: High
3
Content Hierarchy & CopywritingMajor

Hero headline is category-generic for 2026

Hero: "The data & AI platform for modern finance." Subhead: "Spade takes messy transaction data and turns it into structured, verified records — with AI agents that help you use it everywhere it matters."

Why this matters for Spade: "The data & AI platform for X" is the most common B2B headline structure in 2026. It says nothing specific about Spade and could be pasted onto Ntropy, Heron, or any competitor. The subhead does more work but it's carrying weight the headline should be sharing.

COPY-03 Headline Specificity / COPY-02 Benefit-led vs Feature-led

Recommendation

Rewrite the hero around the specific claim Spade actually owns. Direction: "Transaction data enrichment trusted by 1 in 3 U.S. fintech startups." Or: "Turn raw transactions into verified merchant records in <50ms — used by Stripe, Mercury, and Cash App." Lead with the proof.

Effort: LowImpact: High
4
Conversion Rate OptimizationMajor

"Contact sales" is the only path — engineers can't evaluate

Both above-the-fold CTAs are "Contact sales." There is a "Docs" nav link, but it isn't positioned as an alternative conversion path. The result: every visitor is funneled into one buying motion.

Why this matters for Spade: Enterprise infra buying is dominated by engineering-led evaluation. Engineers don't "contact sales" on a first visit — they read docs, try a sandbox, and bring a recommendation to their buyer. A sales-only CTA path loses the engineer segment of the buying committee — exactly the people who turn evaluations into purchase orders.

CRO-04 Engagement Path Variety / CRO-03 CTA Redundancy

Recommendation

Add a secondary CTA: "Read the API docs" or "Request sandbox access." Visually subordinate to "Contact sales" but present above the fold. This captures the engineer-led evaluation path that eventually pulls through to the sales call.

Effort: LowImpact: High
5
Trust SignalsMajor

No visible SOC 2, PCI, or ISO 27001 badges

The footer surfaces Terms, Privacy, MSA, and SLA — but no SOC 2 Type II, ISO 27001, or PCI-DSS badges. For a company processing transaction data for banks and enterprise fintechs, these are table stakes.

Why this matters for Spade: An enterprise procurement or security reviewer will look for these first. If Spade has them (it almost certainly does given the customer list), not displaying them creates unnecessary friction in the deal cycle — and sends engineering-led buyers to a sales call they shouldn't need to have.

CRO-06 Trust Signal Placement

Recommendation

Add a compliance strip in the footer linking to a dedicated Trust/Security page: "SOC 2 Type II · PCI-DSS · ISO 27001" with audit dates, subprocessors, and DPA templates. If only some certifications are current, still display those — transparency wins deals.

Effort: LowImpact: High
