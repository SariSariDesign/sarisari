Evaluation Lens

Notch's whole differentiation is "AI built for regulated industries." Buyers in insurance, banking, and gaming have been burned by generic AI vendors that can't handle compliance, audit trails, and PII. The site's job is to make the compliance story the hero — not an afterthought in the footer.

Why This Matters

Notch is competing in one of the most crowded AI categories of the year where Ada, Sierra, Decagon, and Intercom Fin are fighting for the same buyers. The only way to stand out is to own a niche — "regulated industries" — and make that ownership obvious within 5 seconds of landing.

Top 5 Findings

Ranked by estimated impact on pipeline conversion.

1
Trust SignalsMajor

SOC 2 Type II and ISO 27001 live in the footer — they should live in the hero

Notch is SOC 2 Type II and ISO 27001 certified — the exact proof points that the positioning ("AI Built for Regulated Industries") promises. But both certifications appear only in the footer, meaning the first scroll doesn't deliver on the promise the headline just made.

Why this matters for Notch: For the target persona — a compliance-cautious enterprise buyer in insurance, banking, or gaming — the certifications ARE the value proposition. Every competitor (Ada, Sierra, Decagon) can claim "AI customer support." Only a subset can prove enterprise compliance on day one. Burying that proof wastes your biggest wedge.

CRO-06 Trust Signal Placement / CRO-07 Social Proof Depth

Recommendation

Add a compliance badge strip immediately below the hero: "SOC 2 Type II · ISO 27001 · CCPA compliant · GDPR ready." Link to a dedicated Security & Compliance page with audit dates, subprocessor list, DPA templates, and penetration test summaries. This is your moat — treat it like one.

Effort: LowImpact: High
2
Content Hierarchy & CopywritingMajor

The hero makes the claim — but doesn't show the proof

The hero reads: "AI Built for Regulated Industries" with a subhead of "Designed for the complexity of real-life support. Notch handles regulated workflows, sensitive data, and enterprise compliance at scale." It's a clear positioning claim, but the subhead doubles down on the same abstract promise without naming a single proof point.

Why this matters for Notch: Regulated-industry buyers are professionally skeptical — their job is to find reasons NOT to trust a vendor. A hero that says "trust us, we handle compliance" without naming a certification, vertical, or stat loses those buyers before they scroll. Meanwhile, you already have the proof: SOC 2, ISO 27001, 20M conversations, 30% autonomous resolution in 90 days.

COPY-03 Headline Specificity / COPY-08 Proof & Credibility

Recommendation

Rewrite the subhead to lead with proof: "SOC 2 Type II and ISO 27001 certified. Resolves 30% of tickets autonomously in 90 days. Trusted by operators in insurance, gaming, and finance." Let the facts do the credibility work the adjectives can't.

Effort: LowImpact: High
3
Content Hierarchy & CopywritingMajor

Your three strongest stats are buried below the fold

Notch has three exceptionally strong quantitative claims — "20M conversations handled," "70% cost reduction," and "30% of tickets autonomously resolved within 90 days" — but they appear as a mid-page stat strip rather than anchoring the hero.

Why this matters for Notch: In a category where every competitor promises "AI that resolves tickets," specific numbers are the only defensible proof. A buyer who bounces after reading the hero never sees them — and bounces because the hero didn't give them a reason to stay.

COPY-04 Information Hierarchy / CRO-05 Value Proposition Clarity

Recommendation

Promote "30% of tickets autonomously resolved within 90 days" into the hero as a proof line directly below the headline. Keep the remaining two as a stat strip immediately below. This single change converts a generic category claim into a defensible product truth.

Effort: LowImpact: High
4
Conversion Rate OptimizationMajor

No mid-funnel asset for compliance officers, security reviewers, and procurement

Every CTA on the site leads to the same destination: "Book a demo." There's no gated case study, ROI calculator, compliance whitepaper, or vendor comparison — nothing for a buyer who isn't yet ready to book a 30-minute sales call.

Why this matters for Notch: Regulated-industry buying committees take 3-6 months and involve 5-10 stakeholders. Compliance officers and security reviewers — the people who can veto a deal — will not book a demo until they've independently vetted the vendor. Without a mid-funnel capture, Notch has no way to identify those evaluators or nurture them. The only leads you capture are the 5% already ready to buy.

CRO-03 CTA Redundancy / CRO-04 Engagement Path Variety

Recommendation

Publish a gated "Compliance Brief: AI Customer Support in Regulated Industries" covering SOC 2, ISO 27001, data residency, PII handling, and audit trails. This becomes both your mid-funnel lead magnet and your sales collateral — and it gives security reviewers a reason to give you their email months before a demo.

Effort: MediumImpact: High
5
Conversion Rate OptimizationMajor

Six verticals, one generic CTA — the demo moment is wasted

The nav lists 6 vertical solutions (Ecommerce, SaaS, Gaming, Insurance, Banking & Finance, Travel), but every CTA on the homepage is the generic "Book a demo." Even on vertical-specific solution pages, the CTA doesn't capture which vertical the visitor came from.

Why this matters for Notch: An insurance buyer and a gaming operator have completely different compliance concerns, ticket types, and ROI models. A generic demo CTA fails to signal "we've solved YOUR specific problem" at the moment of highest intent — and means the first sales call starts by asking questions the website should have captured.

CRO-04 Engagement Path Variety / CRO-02 CTA Specificity

Recommendation

On vertical solution pages, swap the CTA to "See Notch for Insurance" (or the relevant vertical) and pre-fill that vertical in the demo form. At minimum, capture vertical + priority use case in the form so the sales call is tailored from minute one. Also group the nav dropdown visually as "Regulated" (Insurance, Banking, Gaming) vs "Commerce" (Ecommerce, SaaS, Travel) — reinforce your wedge even in the navigation.

Effort: MediumImpact: High
