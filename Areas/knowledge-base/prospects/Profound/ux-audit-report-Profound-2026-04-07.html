Methodology

This evaluation assessed tryprofound.com across 9 UX dimensions using automated accessibility and SEO audits, DOM inspection, content analysis, and structured heuristic evaluation against enterprise SaaS best practices.

Evaluation Lens

This audit is framed through the lens of a Series B enterprise SaaS company defining a new category (Answer Engine Optimization). At this stage, the website must simultaneously educate the market about the problem, establish credibility against well-funded competitors, and efficiently convert high-intent enterprise buyers into demo requests.

Why This Matters

A VP of Marketing evaluating Profound alongside SEMrush or Ahrefs will make a snap judgment based on design quality, messaging clarity, and social proof within 10 seconds. The current homepage has structural issues that undermine all three.

Top 5 Findings

Selected for business impact, evidence quality, and relevance to Profound's growth stage and GTM model.

1
Conversion Rate Optimization Critical

Scroll-Triggered Animations Hide ~80% of Homepage Content

The homepage uses JavaScript-driven scroll animations to reveal content as users scroll. When these animations don't fire — which happens on slow connections, with script-blocking browser extensions, for screen readers, or for search engine crawlers — visitors see a massive black void between the hero section and footer. Out of a 9,281px page height, only the hero (~800px) renders without JavaScript animation triggers. DOM inspection confirmed 79 elements with visibility:hidden that depend entirely on scroll position to become visible.

Why this matters for Profound: Enterprise buyers evaluating your platform won't scroll through empty black space — they'll bounce. Equally damaging, search engine and AI crawlers (the exact audience Profound helps brands reach) cannot index 80% of the homepage content. For a company defining the AEO category, this means the homepage can't rank for the educational terms that drive top-of-funnel awareness. The irony is significant: Profound helps brands be visible to AI, but its own website is largely invisible to crawlers.

CRO-01 Above-the-Fold Clarity    ACC-03 Meaningful Sequence    SEO-12 Page Indexability

Recommendation

Restructure the homepage so all content is visible by default. Use progressive enhancement: add scroll animations as a visual enhancement for capable browsers, but render content without JavaScript dependency. Replace visibility:hidden with CSS-only transitions (e.g., opacity via Intersection Observer with a no-JS fallback) that degrade gracefully. Validate by disabling JavaScript and confirming all sections are readable.

Effort: Medium Impact: Critical
2
Content Hierarchy & Copywriting Major

Homepage Messaging Assumes Market Already Understands AEO

The homepage never defines what Answer Engine Optimization is or why it matters. The hero reads "Marketing agents to win in [rotating AI platform names]" with a subheadline about reaching consumers using AI — but there's no explanation of the problem Profound solves, how AEO differs from traditional SEO, or why a VP of Marketing should care about this shift now. The term "Answer Engine Optimization" appears only once across the entire homepage, with no definition.

Why this matters for Profound: Profound is a category-defining company. That means the website's first job isn't selling the product — it's selling the problem. A VP of Marketing currently spending $200K/year on SEMrush needs to understand why AEO is a separate, urgent problem before they'll evaluate any solution. Without that education moment on the homepage, the sales team carries the full burden of market education in every discovery call, which extends the sales cycle and limits pipeline velocity.

COPY-01 5-Second Test    COPY-02 Benefit-Led vs. Feature-Led Framing    COPY-04 Audience Clarity

Recommendation

Add a "The Problem" section immediately below the hero that contrasts traditional search with AI-generated answers. Define AEO in plain language: what it is, why it's different from SEO, and what's at stake for brands that ignore it. Lead with a market statistic (e.g., the "100M+ daily AI searchers" already on the page) and connect it to a pain point the target persona already feels. This section should be the bridge between "I'm curious" and "I need this."

Effort: Medium Impact: High
3
Conversion Rate Optimization Major

Social Proof Section Relies on a Single Testimonial

The homepage social proof section contains one testimonial from Ramp's VP of Growth and a link to a case study. The enterprise page references Ramp (7x visibility increase), Airbyte (tripled in one week), and press mentions from Fortune, WSJ, TechCrunch, and others — but none of this appears on the homepage where conversion decisions are made. Enterprise metrics (1B+ citations, 30B+ crawler visits) are presented without context, making them meaningless to a first-time visitor.

Why this matters for Profound: Enterprise buyers evaluate vendors in comparison. SEMrush displays hundreds of customer logos and detailed case studies with ROI metrics on their homepage. Profound showing a single quote from one customer creates a trust gap that's especially damaging for a younger company asking enterprises to adopt a new category. The Ramp and Airbyte results are compelling — they just aren't where prospects can see them during their evaluation.

CRO-06 Trust Signal Placement    CRO-07 Social Proof Depth    COPY-11 Social Proof Copy

Recommendation

Surface the strongest proof points onto the homepage: add a customer logo bar (Ramp, Airbyte, and others), inline 2-3 case study cards with quantified results (e.g., "Ramp: 7x AI visibility increase in 90 days"), and add the media logo bar (Fortune, WSJ, TechCrunch) near the primary CTA. Position social proof at decision points — after bold claims and directly before the demo request CTA — not in a separate section that users may never reach.

Effort: Medium Impact: High
4
Content Hierarchy & Copywriting Major

Rotating Hero Headline Slows Comprehension of Core Value Proposition

The H1 reads "Marketing agents to win in" followed by rotating AI platform names (Perplexity, ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Grok, etc.). The text changes automatically while the user is reading, creating visual movement that competes with message comprehension. The rotating element also implies that Profound's value differs by platform, when the core value is platform-agnostic. On each visit, the user sees a different completion to the headline, reducing memorability and making it harder to articulate what Profound does.

Why this matters for Profound: For a company asking enterprises to adopt a new category, the headline has roughly 3 seconds to communicate a clear value proposition. Animation in the H1 trades clarity for visual novelty. When a VP of Marketing shares the site with their team in Slack, the preview will show an incomplete headline. When they try to recall what Profound does from memory, the rotating text means they have no single phrase to anchor to.

COPY-01 5-Second Test    COPY-03 Headline Specificity    N-01 Visibility of System Status

Recommendation

Replace the rotating headline with a single, static value proposition that can stand on its own. Use the platform logos as a supporting visual element below the headline (a logo bar or carousel) to demonstrate breadth without sacrificing clarity. Test options like: "Own your brand's visibility in every AI answer" or "The platform for Answer Engine Optimization." A strong H1 should be quotable, scannable, and complete in a single read.

Effort: Low Impact: High
5
Accessibility Major

Multiple Accessibility Gaps: No Skip Nav, No Form Labels, Broken Heading Hierarchy

The homepage has three compounding accessibility issues. First, there is no skip-navigation link, forcing keyboard and screen reader users to tab through 100+ navigation links before reaching content. Second, both form fields on the page use placeholder-only labels with no associated <label> elements — screen readers cannot announce the field purpose. Third, the heading hierarchy is broken: the page has two H1 tags (hero and conference promo) and skips from H2 directly to H4 in the conference section, bypassing H3 entirely.

Why this matters for Profound: Enterprise procurement increasingly includes accessibility compliance as a vendor requirement. A prospect's accessibility team running a quick audit would flag these issues immediately, potentially disqualifying Profound before the sales team even gets a call. Beyond compliance, these issues affect anyone using assistive technology, keyboard navigation, or even just tabbing through a form on mobile — which includes a meaningful share of the enterprise buyer population.

ACC-16 Bypass Blocks    ACC-26 Labels or Instructions    SEO-04 Heading Hierarchy    ACC-02 Info and Relationships

Recommendation

Three targeted fixes: (1) Add a skip-navigation link as the first focusable element on the page, visually hidden until focused. (2) Add explicit <label> elements to all form fields, programmatically associated via the for attribute. (3) Demote the "Zero Click" conference section from H1 to H2, and restructure the conference sub-headings (San Francisco, New York City) from H4 to H3. These are low-effort changes that immediately improve compliance posture.

Effort: Low Impact: Medium
